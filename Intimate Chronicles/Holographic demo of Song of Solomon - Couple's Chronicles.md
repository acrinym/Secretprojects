## **THE GARDEN BLOOMS**

*A Story for Solomon & His Beloved*

---

You smell it before you see it—the heavy sweetness of pomegranates blooming in the evening air, mixed with something sharper, spiced. Myrrh. Your hands pause in arranging the mandrakes you've gathered, their roots still dusty from the earth. He'll know what they mean. He always does.

The winter felt endless this time. Even after the rains stopped, even after the almond blossoms appeared white against the hillsides, the cold lingered. You watched the garden from the window of your chamber, waiting for the moment when you'd know. When the earth would be ready. When you would be ready.

Today, when you walked through the vineyard and saw the first pomegranate bud split open, crimson against green, you knew.

The garden is yours—yours together. Not the formal courtyards with their fountains and marble benches where courtiers stroll and servants trim hedges into submission. This is wild. The pomegranate trees sprawl unpruned, heavy with blooms now, their branches low enough to touch. The myrrh grows in the corner where no one tended it, thriving on neglect and privacy. The mandrakes you planted yourself, on your knees in the dirt, while he watched and smiled at your concentration.

This is where you're most yourselves.

You finish arranging the mandrakes near the stone bench—casual, as if they grew there naturally, as if you didn't place them exactly where he'd see them first. The scent rises in the warmth of late afternoon sun: earthy and sweet and ancient, carrying promises that don't need to be spoken.

You remember the first time he brought you to a banquet hall—you, the vineyard worker with sun-darkened skin, walking beside royalty. The way everyone stared. But he'd looked at you like you were the only person in the room, and when he'd said, "My banner over you is love," you'd believed him. You believe him still.

His footsteps on the path are unmistakable—measured, purposeful, but quickening as he rounds the last corner. You don't turn yet. Let him find you here, among the blooming trees, with the light going golden and the air thick with spices.

"There you are."

His voice. After weeks of formality and court business and sleeping in separate quarters because visiting dignitaries required propriety, his voice saying *there you are* like you're the destination he's been traveling toward.

You turn.

He stops at the edge of the garden, and you watch him take it in: the pomegranates blooming, the myrrh releasing its fragrance in the evening heat, the mandrakes arranged with care. His eyes find you last, and when they do, something in his expression shifts.

"My dove," he says softly. "My perfect one."

The words are familiar—he's called you this before—but tonight they land differently. Tonight they sound like *I've missed you* and *I'm home* and *winter is finally past*.

You don't move yet. You want to watch him cross the garden to you, want to see his hands reach for you before you feel them. Want the anticipation to stretch just a little longer, because you've learned that the ache of wanting makes the having sweeter.

He comes to you slowly, as if he knows exactly what you're doing, as if he's complicit in drawing this out. His eyes don't leave yours, but you see him notice everything else too: the way the evening breeze catches your hair, the olive oil you rubbed into your skin until it gleams, the particular dress you chose—the one he once said made you look like a lily among thorns.

"The garden is blooming," he says when he reaches you, close enough now that you can smell the cedar and wine on him, the particular scent that means *him*.

"I know," you say. "I've been waiting."

"For the pomegranates?"

"For spring."

He smiles—that private smile that's only for you, the one that transforms his face from regal composure into something unguarded and young. "Winter is past," he says, and you know he's quoting your own words back to you from that first reunion, when you'd opened your door to find him there after weeks apart, and all you could say was, "The winter is past, the rains are over and gone."

"The flowers appear on the earth," you continue the verse, stepping into the space between you.

His hand comes up to your face, thumb tracing the line of your jaw. "Dark," he says, "and lovely."

You lean into his palm. How many times has he said this? That your sun-darkened skin is beautiful, not something to apologize for? Enough times that you've started to believe it.

"I arranged the mandrakes," you say.

His eyes flicker to where they lie, and his smile deepens. "I noticed."

"I thought you might."

"Did you." It's not a question. His hand slides from your jaw to the back of your neck, fingers tangling in your hair. "And what did you think I would do, when I noticed?"

Your breath catches. This—this is the part you love. The moment when longing sharpens into something immediate, when the ache of waiting transforms into the urgency of *now*.

"I thought," you say, and your voice comes out softer than you intended, "you might be pleased."

"Pleased." He tastes the word, considers it. "Yes. Though that seems too mild a word for what I feel, finding you here, in our garden, with the pomegranates blooming and the mandrakes laid out like an invitation."

He pulls you closer, slow and deliberate, until you're pressed against him and you can feel his heartbeat against your chest—steady and strong and quickening.

"Tell me," you breathe, because you need to hear it, always need to hear it.

"You are beautiful," he says, his voice low and certain. "Altogether beautiful, my darling. There is no flaw in you."

The words sink into you like rain into parched ground. This is what you came to the garden for—not just the pleasure of skin and touch, but this: being seen, being known, being chosen again and again.

His mouth finds yours, and the kiss is slow at first, deliberate, tasting of wine and promise. But then his hand tightens in your hair, and your hands find his shoulders, and the kiss deepens into something that's been building for weeks.

When you finally break apart, both breathing harder, he rests his forehead against yours.

"I've missed you," he says.

"You've seen me every day."

"Not like this." His hands are moving now, one cupping your face, the other at the small of your back, pulling you closer. "Not *you*. The vineyard worker who put mandrakes in the garden and looks at me like I'm not a king."

"You're not," you say. "Not here."

"No." He kisses your temple, your cheekbone, the corner of your mouth. "Here I'm just a man who's been waiting all day to get his beloved alone."

*Beloved*. The word that started as his name for you and became something more. The word that means *chosen*, that means *mine*, that means *yours*.

He guides you backward toward the stone bench, and you let him, trust him, give yourself over to his hands and his attention. The evening air is warm on your skin where he pushes the fabric aside, and the scent of myrrh intensifies as if the garden itself is responding to what's unfolding here.

"Tell me what you want," he says against your neck, and you can feel his smile.

"You know what I want."

"Say it anyway."

This is part of your dance—he makes you ask, makes you voice your desire, because he knows that naming it makes it more real. And because he wants to hear it, wants the affirmation that you want him as much as he wants you.

"You," you say simply. "All of you. Here. Now."

His breath catches, and you feel the shudder that runs through him. "My dove," he murmurs. "My only one. My perfect one."

The garden holds you both as the light fades from gold to amber to the deep blue of early evening. The pomegranates bloom above you, their flowers releasing sweetness into the air. The mandrakes you placed so carefully give up their ancient scent. And in this space—your space—you're not vineyard worker and king, not sun-darkened skin and royal blood, not anything except what you've always been here:

Beloved and lover. Chosen and choosing. Two people who looked at each other once and knew.

Later—much later—when you're both breathing slowly again, his arms around you and your head on his chest, he speaks into the darkness.

"Thank you," he says.

"For what?"

"For waiting for spring. For arranging the mandrakes. For being here."

You smile against his skin. "Where else would I be?"

"Anywhere." His hand strokes your hair, gentle and reverent. "You could be anywhere. And you chose here. Chose me."

"Always," you say. And mean it.

Above you, the stars are emerging one by one, and the garden breathes its spices into the night. The winter is past. The flowers appear on the earth. And in this moment, in this space that's yours alone, everything is exactly as it should be.

His banner over you is love.

And you are home.
